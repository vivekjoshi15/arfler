We all want to live lives that are happy. We all want to be healthy. We all want to be productive. Yet happiness seems elusive. Being healthy seems like a lot of work and our lives are busy enough as it is. And we are not as productive as we would like to be. How do we achieve this seemingly impossible goal? What are we to do? Luckily, it is not as complicated or hard as it seems. There is a simple and easy way to live a life that is healthy, happy, and productive. In this insightful book, Harry Mokm share his five simple steps to do this. It is called BETAS.

It is Breathing-Eating-Thinking-Action-Sleeping. By making simple yet effective changes in these five areas we can live lives that are happy and healthy and yes, productive!

Harry Mokm has lived all over the globe and has picked up wisdom wherever he has lived and this book is full of these global insights. For instance, eating healthy does not have to mean giving up pleasure. Healthy eating can actually be enjoyable. There are foods from across the globe from Japan to Greece that are good tasting and good for you.

It is not hard. Harry shows just how simple and easy it actually is to live a happy, healthy, and productive life. By implementing these easy to implement changes we can achieve our goals and help create the better world we all would like to see.

Capitalism. It has created unimaginable wealth. It has helped lift countless millions out of poverty. It has enabled people to live the lives of freedom and prosperity that they have dreamed of. This is what Adam Smith had envisioned when he wrote his groundbreaking book, The Wealth of Nations, back in 1776. Our founding fathers, including Thomas Jefferson, loved the book. In fact, Jefferson considered it the best book on economics ever written. Alexander Hamilton, the first Secretary of the Treasury, used Smith’s ideas when he founded the American economy. The book was revolutionary for its time and now Smith’s ideas are now commonplace. Capitalism is perhaps the best economic system ever invented. Yet there are flaws.

The system has been corrupted. There is a growing disparity in wealth as the gap between the rich and the poor continues to widen. The middle class is beginning to disappear. Big corporations and well-paid lobbyists are taking advantage of the system for their own exclusive benefit leaving the consumer to foot the bill. Our economy is becoming more and more unequal. We have won the Cold War but we are still under threat from a new mutated Communism, not from Europe, but from a more toxic and dangerous form in China which is a growing threat to the West.

In essence, the issue with capitalism is the human factor. Adam Smith foresaw this and worried about it. Yet nothing has been done regarding this. If we are going to survive, then something needs to change. We need to reinvent our economic system and update capitalism, a version 2.0. In this insightful and groundbreaking work, entrepreneur Harry Mokm describes this new system he calls iCapitalism. In this system, the problems with capitalism are fixed by establishing control mechanisms which prevents corruption and people from taking advantage of the system and helps control the distribution of wealth so that it is both fair and equitable.

For anyone interested in economics or history and for anyone who wants to see things change for the better, this book is a must read.

In the aftermath, Harry is gathering the remnants of the Andrew Wang campaign which included Universal Basic Income, the old Bernie Sanders supporters, Greens, Moderate Democrats, and even some libertarians around his vision. They are all at a convention to draft a new Democratic Party platform centered around a concept that has grown increasingly popular, “iCapitalism”.

Second scene is a flashback that Harry has triggered by his attempt to explain how iCapitalism goes beyond the Cold War mentality of capitalism vs. communism. He flashes back to his first days following birth, when he was placed under a starfruit tree in Vietnam (starfruit can be a metaphor for iCapitalism in that the five “arms” of the starfruit can represent individuality and uniqueness, while the core of the starfruit represents the collective context that provides a basis from which individuality can be expressed in its highest capacities and greatest expression of merit (meritocracy). A narrator’s “voice” explains the whole context and lays the groundwork for the novel by comparing a series of conflicting factors, especially economic conflicts between Global Capitalism and Global Communism, between The East and The West, and once he arrives in North America, between Canada (social democracy with a welfare state) and the United States (casino capitalism, neoliberal capitalism, etc.).
